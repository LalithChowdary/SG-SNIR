Issue 4 — HIGH: Experiment 3 Soc-Epinions1 Result Is Presented Incorrectly
The paper says:

"On soc-Epinions1 (k=10, 1 trial), SG-SNIR uses 286 evaluations vs. 445 for exhaustive search, a 1.56× speedup. The quality gap is +14.1%"

Several problems here. First, 1 trial is explicitly flagged as "indicative rather than definitive" which is acceptable but the +14.1% quality gap is alarming and needs context. A 14.1% quality gap is much larger than the 0.065–2.256% gaps seen elsewhere. This will concern a reviewer without explanation. Second, this contradicts Experiment 2 which shows soc-Epinions1 having 601 SG evals and 790 MEH evals — different from the 286 and 445 cited here. The numbers are inconsistent between experiments and need to be reconciled.

Issue 5 — HIGH: DINO Comparison Methodology Needs Clearer Framing
The Experiment 9 description says:

"We subjected both algorithms to massive, globally distributed epidemics by initializing the SNIR simulation with 300 and 600 random seed nodes"

But elsewhere the paper uses 5 seed nodes as the standard configuration. Jumping to 300 and 600 seeds specifically for the DINO comparison without explaining why will confuse a reviewer. Add one sentence explaining:

"We use large seed counts (300, 600) for this comparison to create outbreak conditions where DINO's node removal strategy selects meaningful targets — with only 5 seeds the KSCC containment effect is too small to differentiate the methods clearly."

Also the paper says "quarantining up to 15 central nodes severed exactly 1,256 edges" — this is a very specific number and a reviewer will ask how it was computed. Add one sentence clarifying that this is the sum of in-degree plus out-degree of the 15 selected nodes in the original graph.
